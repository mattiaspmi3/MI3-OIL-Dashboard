Oil & Gas 101

A plain-English primer for newcomers and investors — what the industry is, how the oil actually gets out of the ground, and why it moves markets.

The business, in one minute

The oil & gas industry finds hydrocarbons underground, brings them to the surface, moves them, and turns them into fuels and products. It runs in three stages:

Upstream — producing

Exploration & production (E&P): finding oil/gas and drilling wells to bring it up. This is where “basins,” “shale,” and the companies on this dashboard (ExxonMobil, ConocoPhillips, EOG, Chord, Diamondback…) operate.

Midstream — moving & storing

Pipelines, gathering systems, storage, processing plants and export terminals (including LNG terminals that chill gas to a liquid for shipping overseas). Midstream is why a pipeline-constrained basin like Appalachia can’t always sell all it produces.

Downstream — refining & selling

Refineries turn crude into gasoline, diesel and jet fuel; marketers sell it. The Gulf Coast is the U.S. refining and export hub — a big advantage for nearby basins like the Eagle Ford.

How shale oil actually gets out of the ground

Conventional vs. shale (tight) oil

Conventional oil sits in a porous pocket and flows up a vertical well — like sipping a juice box. Shale (tight) oil is locked inside dense rock that won’t flow on its own; you have to crack the rock open first.

Horizontal drilling + fracking

The oil-rich rock layer is thin but spreads sideways for miles. Modern wells drill down, then turn horizontal and run 2–4 miles through that layer (“lateral feet”). Then hydraulic fracturing (“fracking”) pumps water and sand (“proppant”) at high pressure to crack the rock; the sand props the cracks open so oil can flow.

Why “lateral feet” is the key metric

More rock contacted = more oil. The length of horizontal wellbore drilled is the headline productivity number analysts track.

Rock tiers & “high-grading”

Acreage is graded Tier 1 (best) to Tier 4. Companies drill their best rock first (“high-grading”), so the leftover inventory gets progressively worse — quality of remaining locations matters more than the raw count.

Pressure depletion

Wells drain a shared reservoir. A new well drilled too close or too late to older wells hits already-drained rock and produces less. As a basin matures, more new wells hit depleted rock — a key reason a basin’s best days are finite.

Why it matters to an investor

Energy is only ~3–4% of the S&P 500 by weight, but oil & gas ripple through the whole economy via gasoline prices, inflation, and transport costs. The U.S. is now the world’s top producer of both oil and gas. The single biggest shift to understand: oil is maturing toward a plateau while natural gas is the growth engine (LNG exports + AI/data-center power demand).
